## Post 1

You can turn your marketing team into an 'AI powerhouse' by simply replicating how David Ogilvy worked.

David Ogilvy was obsessive about writing down his judgment. Every hard-won lesson about what makes advertising work went into memos that circulated across every Ogilvy & Mather office. When he retired in the 1970s, the agency didn't lose its standards — because he'd spent 25 years capturing his judgment in writing.

This same practice is how you make AI way more impactful for your teams.

Create an internal knowledge base and have teams write down their learnings, what works, what doesn't, and why they do the things they do. That knowledge base is today's leverage. An intelligence layer, unique to you, that can be plugged into AI models and agents.

This is part of the 3-step AI strategy all marketing teams should begin to adopt:
- Measure AI's impact on real outcomes for my business
- Teach AI on my team's raw intelligence
- Create a knowledge base of lessons, ways of working, and real intelligence about how things work, readable by both AI and humans

The Ogilvy method.

## Post 2

One of the best skills to learn is how to 'disagree' without being a 'd*ck'.

In a lot of companies, everyone takes offense at their stuff being questioned, people take it personally, and eventually no one disagrees — everything is done by reaching consensus and being nice to each other. A good example: the most popular managers are often the least effective.

A couple of rituals to help:

1. Don't let people just add non-stop open-ended questions to memos/docs/strategy presentations. You have to express a point of view. The people who show up constantly with open-ended questions, vs. just saying what they mean, slow everything down.

2. We have a meeting ritual that requires a DRI to present a clear recommendation on any decision or blocker. They state the problem, 3 potential solutions, one recommendation, and a clear why. If there is disagreement, get into a room, debate, and then ship a decision immediately.

Disagreements with clear reasons is a superpower. You give real context, show you've understood the problem, and have clear push back.

The number one rule in the art of disagreeing: don't be a 'd*ck'.

## Post 4

Ok, Fable 5 is out. Your feed is full of buzzwords — "insane", "game changing", "everything is dead". But if you're a marketer, what can you actually do with it?

Fable 5 is for big, meaty, multi-hour problems where you give it a goal and leave it to do lots of work. Here are example use cases where you'll get real value:

1. Mining your customer data for the one insight that matters. Feed it thousands of survey responses, CRM notes, call transcripts, and chat logs. Fable has an edge because of how much context it can hold and synthesize.

2. Multi-source GTM research in a single pass. Ask it to pull your analytics, walk through your pricing page, review competitor reviews on G2, and provide a recommendation on how you can improve your positioning.

3. Audit your entire website for messaging consistency in one pass. Ask Fable to read every page on your marketing site and flag where your positioning contradicts itself, where your value props change wording, and where your CTAs don't match the page intent.

Keep your current model if you need: writing copy, quick back-and-forth work, or anything that isn't big, multi-step, large context, multi-task.

## Post 5

The perfect marketing leader today can do two things really well: Scale 'owned audience' + Scale 'AI workflows'.

1. Scale 'owned audience': AI made it easier to ship software products. It made marketing harder than ever. Because AI has taken a sledgehammer to every platform — every channel is being saturated, making it harder for any one thing to get attention.

A Nobel Prize-winning economist predicted this exact problem in 1971. Herbert Simon wrote that a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention. Every time you increase supply, you decrease attention. AI has given us an infinite supply in a world with finite attention.

There are two types of marketing now: you can rent attention on platforms where AI has made supply infinite, or you'll need to build 'owned attention' — a newsletter someone opens, a podcast someone subscribes to, a community, an event.

2. Scale 'AI workflows': AI can allow you to fundamentally rebuild how your marketing team works. Give AI the right context and it can research content, find angles, and do first drafts. Give AI the right data and it can create high-converting outbound and inbound emails. Give AI the right training and it can act as an inbound SDR, automating discovery and qualification.

Scale 'owned audience' + Scale 'AI workflows' = the perfect marketing leader.
