# Richard van der Blom — LinkedIn Posts

## Post 1

Nobody owes you their attention.

I've been saying this since 2016, when most people on LinkedIn treated their audience like a captive crowd. 80% pitch. 20% substance. As if followers had nowhere else to scroll.

Ten years later? Same mistake. Different excuses.

"But I need the business." "My boss wants visibility." "I don't have time for value posts."

Meanwhile, the algorithm got smarter. The feed got more demanding and trust became the scarcest currency on the platform.

The people still treating their audience like they owe them something? Their posts sink. Their DMs go unanswered. Their connection requests sit in limbo.

The ones who are willing to invest time? They're getting messages like "I've been reading your stuff for months." They build pipeline without cold outreach. They attract instead of chase.

I've trained over 350,000 professionals on this. The data is clear and the pattern is undeniable.

When you lead with value, people want to hear what you're offering. When you pitch first, they scroll past before your second line loads.

So before you hit publish, ask yourself: Is this worth my scroll? If the answer is no, don't expect it from anyone else.

## Post 2

LinkedIn just made a small but smart change. In your feed, engagement numbers now show as symbols instead of text.

👍 180 💬 144 🔁 2

Clean. Fast. Less cluttered. Good move.

But since we're already tweaking the engagement experience, here are 5 features that would actually change how creators use this platform:

1. Show me who saved my post — Saves are your strongest reach signal. A post with 10+ saves reaches a median of 2,085% more than one with zero. Yet you hide who saved it completely.

2. Show me engagement streaks — Who has liked, commented or shared 3, 5, 10 of my posts in a row? That person is not a passive follower. That's a warm lead. Possibly a buyer.

3. Show me multiple-action engagers — Who liked AND commented AND shared the same post? That triple signal is the highest intent a follower can show without sending a DM.

4. Suggest connections based on the above — Start recommending people who have been quietly telling me they're interested. Turn the algorithm into a sales assistant.

5. Show me which companies are engaging repeatedly — Not just individuals. Which organisations are sending multiple people to my content? That's account-level intent data sitting unused. B2B creators would pay for that insight alone.

Bonus: Let me see engagement trends over time per follower — Did someone go from liking one post a month to three a week? Show me the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

LinkedIn already has this data. The gap between what they track and what they share with creators is still huge.

## Post 3

Most companies are leaving 80% of their LinkedIn reach on the table. Not because they're inactive, but because they're following outdated rules.

They invest in the Company Page, hire someone to post 3x a week and then wonder why only 200 people see it.

After analysing millions of posts for the Algorithm Insights Report 2026, this is what we see:

→ Personal profiles get 8x more reach and 12x the engagement of Company Pages. Pages speak into the void. People interact.

→ "Resharing with your own thoughts" is a no-go. LinkedIn classifies this as low-effort copied content and suppresses it. What works: comment on the Company Page post, or use instant reshare.

→ Your thought leaders are your highest-value distribution channel. B2B buyers follow people, not logos.

→ C-level creating original content get up to 5x more views. A single post from your CEO can reach more qualified buyers in 48 hours than a month of Company Page posts.

→ Who engages matters as much as how many. 80%+ engagement from internal colleagues is an algorithmic red flag. External engagement is the accelerator.

## Post 4

I called my dad yesterday for Father's Day. One day too late. He won't mind.

In less than 3 months I will be a father myself. Exciting and terrifying at the same time.

Four years. Two lost pregnancies. Medical complications. Conversations late at night about whether to keep going, whether this was still worth it, whether we were still okay. We kept going.

When I shared those experiences publicly, over 200 DMs came in. People I had never met, going through the same thing. That moment reminded me why I talk about the hard stuff.

I was 27 when I met my ex-wife. She had two boys, three and seven years old. For seventeen years I tried to be a good stepfather. They taught me patience, presence, showing up even when you don't know if you're doing it right.

September will change everything. Three of us. Four if you count Siena, our thirteen year old labradoodle who has no idea what is coming.

My dad? The thing about dementia nobody tells you, is that it sometimes comes with a laugh. Every time I remind him he is becoming a grandfather at 80, it is the first time he hears it. And every single time, he cannot stop cheering.

I get to give him that moment twice a day. Life is not clean or linear. But it is worth showing up for.

## Post 5

It was 1999. My first job, at a staffing agency. A major Dutch car factory put their staffing contract out to tender. 10 agencies invited to pitch. All human to human. Three rounds. Months of work. That's how B2B used to work. People met people, and the best relationship-builder usually won the deal.

Fast forward to last week, when I watched two AI agents negotiate a vendor contract. No human in the room. Pricing, terms, back-and-forth — all handled machine-to-machine, in about 4 minutes.

We've spent 25 years optimizing GTM for human attention. All of it built on one assumption: a person reads it, feels something, decides. That assumption is starting to break.

→ Discovery is moving from search and ads to protocol-native distribution agents can parse
→ Trust is moving from brand and design to machine-readable reputation
→ Funnels are becoming autonomous journeys, no human required to move stage to stage
→ Revenue itself is becoming a recursive execution loop, not a quarterly motion

Most of us are still optimizing for a buyer who reads, feels, and decides like I did in that boardroom in 1999. The next buyer might not read at all.

I'm not saying humans disappear from B2B — high-stakes decisions still need someone accountable. I'm saying the path to that decision is being rebuilt by agents, right now, while most of us are still A/B testing subject lines.
