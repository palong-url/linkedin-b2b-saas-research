## Post 2

Someone asked me why I post my marketing insights and POV so freely. Why I don't do more to capture leads.

Honestly... I didn't have a great answer for it. I finally just said, "Because... I think more people should just care about doing marketing thoughtfully, and I care more about making that impact than getting the form fill."

Do I want more followers? Sure. More newsletter subscribers, more podcast listeners, sure sure. More SparkToro customers? Yes, please. And more book buyers? YES.

But really, I just believe in this:
- Getting and sustaining attention is super hard, and it's only getting harder.
- Big Tech scrapes all your content anyway, AI is only accelerating this, and it sucks for us but in the meantime we would be wise to optimize for impressions and ongoing engagement.
- It's becoming more and more important to know where your audience is actually hanging out online, what they care about, what they search for, and what topics they're interested in... so you can do marketing that actually resonates with them.

Ultimately, I care more about getting people to believe and practice these things than I do about squeezing a conversion out of every idea I share.

So yes, please buy my book and buy a SparkToro account. But also... it's ok if you don't. Just do your best to do thoughtful marketing, make smart bets, be good to your customers, unclench your jaw, and go drink some water.

## Post 3

Even when AI is wrong about you, you're still getting a useful data point.

When I ran SparkToro through an AI visibility tool, it compared us to Ahrefs, Semrush, Similarweb, Brandwatch, and others. My first instinct: That's wrong. SparkToro isn't an SEO suite or enterprise social listening tool.

But it's more useful to ask: *why* did AI get it wrong?

Positioning isn't only what you say on your homepage. It's the context the broader market creates around you: what category you're in, what you replace, who you're for, and what buyers should compare you against. AI systems absorb that context from across the internet.

So don't let AI redefine your positioning. But don't dismiss its mistakes as mere hallucinations, either. Ask what might have produced the association.

Is your category unclear? Do buyers see those tools as alternatives? Are third-party articles, reviews, and comparison pages sending mixed signals?

AI visibility isn't just a score. It's a reflection of your public record.

## Post 4

"How would you start a marketing strategy from scratch?"

The annoying but true answer is: it depends. But because that's less fun, I would start with one question: How is this business supposed to grow?

Is the intended growth mechanism raw discovery? Then SEO, comparison pages, and educational content probably matter a lot.

Is it product-led growth? Then onboarding content, use-case content, templates, lifecycle emails, and activation loops probably matter more.

Is it sales-led? Then the first content priority might be the sales asset the team has been begging for, like a buyer guide or follow-up email sequence.

After this, I would look for the shortest path to proving marketing can help:
- Discovery-led motion → fix the highest-intent pages
- Sales-led motion → create the asset sales needs this month
- Product-led motion → improve the content that shortens time-to-value

Early on, marketing needs to earn internal trust. The fastest way to do that is to understand how the company grows, then make that motion work better.

## Post 5

Everyone got told to 10x their content with AI. So now every other post reads like it was steeped with the same lukewarm teabag. Same structure. Same "It's not this. It's that." Same nothing.

They're no match for lived experience.

A large language model is a phenomenal line cook. Hand it a recipe that already exists online and it'll plate the dish faster and cleaner than you can. What it can't do is taste.

It wasn't on that customer call this morning. It didn't watch the feature flop and ruminate on why. It has no idea what it actually feels like to do the job — because it has never once done the job.

The person who's hands-on with your product, talking to customers, running the experiments — that's a goldmine. Their point of view isn't published anywhere else. It's all firsthand.

Which means the most defensible content input you have is the people already on your payroll.
