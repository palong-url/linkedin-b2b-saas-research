# Matt Ragland — LinkedIn Posts

## Post 1

A coach I talked to recently was pricing her one-on-one at $1,000/month, open-ended. She couldn't figure out why nobody was biting.

Here's the issue. When you sell open-ended coaching, the buyer has no box to put it in. What am I getting? When is it over? How do I know it worked? They don't say any of that out loud. They just don't buy.

Here's the swap I told her: Don't sell coaching. Sell a six-week program with a clear outcome, three working sessions, and a deliverable at the end. Same price. Same access. Totally different decision.

People can wrap their heads around a program. They can't wrap their heads around "working together."

Most coaching offers don't fail because of price. They fail because the buyer can't picture the finish line.

## Post 2

There's a number I watch for when someone tells me Claude isn't working for them. It's how many iterations they did before giving up.

If it's three or four, the problem is usually the prompt. If it's seven, eight, ten... the problem isn't the prompt anymore. The problem is upstream.

It's the context they gave it. Or didn't give it. It's the docs they never built. The voice file they kept meaning to write.

No amount of "actually, make it more like me" fixes a missing foundation.

If you find yourself going in circles, stop iterating. Go back upstream and fix what's missing there.

## Post 3

A client showed me a sales sequence that wasn't converting. I read it. The information was right. The offer was real. The audience knew him.

The problem? He wrote it in someone else's voice. The pumped-up, "you-deserve-this-now" hype voice that works for some people in some markets. It didn't work for his. His list signed up because he wasn't that guy. And then his sales emails tried to make him that guy.

Of course they passed on the offer.

You can have great info, real expertise, and the right offer. If the delivery contradicts the relationship, the sale doesn't happen.

The voice that earned the list is the voice that has to ask for the sale.

## Post 4

I can always tell when a creator is undercharging.

They run a small offer at one price. It does fine. People actually like it. And before the next round, they tell me they're "maybe" going to raise it. Not £500 more. Not £200 more. Like, fifty quid.

The pricing flinch isn't loud anymore. It's just very small.

If your offer worked at £975 with no marketing follow-up, no automation, no slick funnel — you don't have a pricing problem. You have a permission problem.

Charge the next price you're scared of. That's the real one.

## Post 5

There are two reasons to tag a subscriber.

1. To organize them.
2. To target them.

These look the same, but they're not.

Organizing means: I want a record of who bought what, who came in through which lead magnet, who attended the workshop.

Targeting means: I'm going to send this group of people an email the other 12,000 will not get.

Almost every creator I audit has 200+ tags built for organizing and uses about 6 for targeting. The other 194 are just visual clutter.

When you're deciding whether to keep a tag, ask one question: "Will I ever send only this group an email?"

If yes, keep it. If no, that information already exists somewhere else. Your subscriber's purchase history. Their forms. Their automation entry.

You don't need a tag for everything you want to remember. You need a tag for what you want to act on.
